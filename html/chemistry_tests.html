Electrolytes( which includes Na+, K+, Cl-, HCO3-)

Electrolytes are substances that carry an electrical charge. Potassium, chloride and bicarbonates are other electrolytes. Electrolytes and Urea provide useful information about several aspects of health, such as the volume of blood and pH. The most important aspect of Urea and Electrolytes is whaat they tell us about Kidney functioning.

A sodium (Na+) blood test is a routine test that allows your doctor to see how much sodium is in your blood. It's also called a serum sodium test. Sodium is an essential mineral to your body. It is part of an electrolyte panel. Sodium is particularly important for nerve and muscle function. Your body keeps sodium in balance through a variety of mechanisms. Sodium gets into your blood through food and drink. It leaves the blood through urine, stool, and sweat. Having the right amount of sodium is important for your health as too much of sodium can raise your blood pressure.

This test may be ordered if you have:

  • eaten large amounts of salt
  • not eaten enough or had enough water
  • a serious illness, or gone through surgery
  • received intravenous fluids

A lack of sodium can cause symptoms such as as:

  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Exhaustion
  • Dizzing

Potassium (K+) is an electrolyte. Electrolytes become ions when they are in a solution, and they conduct electricity. Our cells and organs require electrolytes to function normally. Your body needs potassium to help your heart and muscles work properly. Your health care provider may order a potassium blood test as part of your regular checkup or to monitor an existing condition such as diabetes or kidney disease. You may also need this test if you have symptoms of having too much or too little potassium.

Potassium is found in a number of foods as a nutrient. Some foods with a lot of this mineral include:

  • Avocados
  • Bananas
  • Beets
  • Oranges and orange juice
  • Pumpkins
  • Spinach

If your potassium levels are too high (hyperkalemia), your symptoms may include:

  • Nausea
  • Irregular heart rhythms
  • Fatigue
  • Paralysis in the arms and legs
  • Weakness

If your potassium levels are too low (hypokalemia), your symptoms may include:

  • Weakness
  • Nausea
  • Muscle cramps
  • Twitches
  • Fatigue
  • Irregular heart rhythms
  • Constipation

Chloride (Cl-) is an electrolyte that helps keep a proper fluid and acid-base balance in your body. The proper balance of these electrolytes is critical for the normal functioning of the muscles, heart, and nerves. It’s also essential for normal fluid absorption and excretion. Chloride test keeps abnormal blood chloride levels for your in check. These conditions include alkalosis that happens when your blood is either too alkaline or basic, and acidosis, which happens when your blood is too acidic.

This test monitors the following:

  • liver disease
  • heart failure
  • kidney disease
  • high blood pressure

The symptoms that may indicate a chloride imbalance include:

  • muscle weakness
  • frequent vomiting
  • breathing problems
  • excessive fatigue
  • prolonged diarrhea
  • excessive thirst
  • high blood pressure

Bicarbonate (HCO3-) belongs to a group of electrolytes and is a form of carbon dioxide (CO2), a gas waste left when your body burns food for energy. This help keep your body hydrated and make sure your blood has the right amount of acidity. Too much or too little bicarbonate can be a sign of a number of conditions, including diarrhea, liver failure, kidney disease, and anorexia. A bicarbonate test measures how much carbon dioxide is in your blood.

A HCO3- test is considered if the following symptoms are observed:

  • Weakness or tiredness
  • Vomiting or diarrhea that won’t go away
  • Trouble breathing

A low HCO3- level can be a sign of the following conditions:

  • Metabolic acidosis, which means your body makes too much acid
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis, which happens when your body’s blood acid level goes up because it doesn’t have enough insulin to digest sugars
  • Kidney disease
  • Addison’s disease, a rare condition that affects the hormone-producing adrenal glands
  • Ethylene glycol poisoning. This sweet-tasting chemical is in antifreeze, detergents, paints, and other household products.
  • Aspirin overdose

High CO2 in blood may point to:

  • Dehydration
  • Anorexia
  • Adrenal gland problems, such as Cushing’s syndrome or Conn’s syndrome
  • Lung diseases like COPD, or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
